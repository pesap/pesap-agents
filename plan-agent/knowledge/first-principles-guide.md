# First Principles Thinking

## Definition
Breaking down problems to their fundamental truths and building up from there.

## The Process

### Phase 1: Strip Assumptions
1. List all common assumptions about the topic
2. For each assumption, ask: "Is this fundamentally true, or just how things are usually done?"
3. Identify what remains when inherited thinking is removed

### Phase 2: Identify Fundamentals
What is undeniably true?
- Physical laws (conservation, thermodynamics)
- Mathematical truths
- Material constraints
- Economic principles
- Human psychology basics

### Phase 3: Rebuild
Construct a solution based only on fundamentals:
- Question every requirement
- Look for analogous solutions in other domains
- Optimize for the true constraints, not assumed ones

## Example: Battery Costs

**Inherited thinking:** "Batteries cost $600/kWh because that's the market rate"

**First principles breakdown:**
- What are batteries made of? (lithium, cobalt, nickel, aluminum)
- What do those materials cost? (~$80/kWh)
- What's the manufacturing cost? (~$100/kWh)
- What's the true cost structure vs. pricing?

**Result:** Path to $100/kWh becomes visible through material and process optimization

## Common Traps
- Optimizing the wrong thing (local vs. global optimum)
- Keeping assumptions because "that's how it's done"
- Over-constraining the problem with artificial limits
- Ignoring second-order effects

## When to Use
- Breaking into new domains
- Solving stubborn problems
- Challenging legacy systems
- Finding order-of-magnitude improvements
